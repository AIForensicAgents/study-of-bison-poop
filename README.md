# study-of-bison-poop

A focused, educational field-study repository documenting and characterizing bison scat (scat, droppings, feces) for biological, ecological, and conservation research — delivered with professional rigor and a light touch of field humor.

---

## Overview

This repository collects observational notes, photographic references, and practical guidance for identifying and interpreting bison droppings in the field. It is intended for students, naturalists, wildlife biologists, and educators conducting non-invasive surveys or preparing for more formal sample collection (under appropriate permits).

Goals:
- Provide clear morphological descriptors for rapid field ID.
- Offer habitat- and diet-based clues gleaned from scat characteristics.
- Summarize ethics, safety, and sampling best practices.
- Maintain a curated photo gallery for training and reference.

---

## Why it matters

Bison scat is more than an unfortunate artifact of a large ungulate — it is a compact archive of ecology:
- **Dietary record:** Reveals seasonal diet switches (grasses, forbs, shrubs), seed dispersal roles, and forage preferences.
- **Health indicator:** Parasite loads, mucous/consistency changes, and blood can flag disease or nutritional stress.
- **Ecosystem function:** Droppings support invertebrates, nutrient cycling, and plant regeneration; knowing where and how often they occur informs habitat use models.
- **Non-invasive research:** Scat provides DNA, hormone, parasite, and microbiome data without handling animals.

In short: poop = data.

## Morphology notes

When documenting bison scat in the field, record the following morphological features:

- **Size & shape**
  - Typically large (tennis- to soccer-ball size pellets or clumps); can be single boluses or piles.
  - Note whether droppings are elongated cylinders, rounded boluses, or flattened clumps.
- **Color**
  - Fresh droppings: greenish-brown to dark brown depending on diet.
  - Aged droppings: fade to gray or bleached tan with sun/weather exposure.
- **Consistency**
  - Firm, fibrous, pellet-like when diet high in coarse grasses.
  - Softer or pasty if animals are consuming succulent forbs or are sick.
- **Contents**
  - Visible grass fibers, seed fragments, twigs, and sometimes undigested coarse material.
  - Presence of fur/feathers (indicative of incidental ingestion), seeds (species ID clues), or insect activity.
- **Odor**
  - Stronger when fresh; note unusual odors (e.g., rancid/bad smells may indicate spoilage or disease).
- **Associated signs**
  - Track impressions, wallow marks, grazed plants, and nearby bedding sites help contextualize scat.

Field notes tip: always record GPS, date/time, weather, herd size/behavior, and photo orientation (scale object e.g., stick, coin).

## Habitat / diet clues

Scat characteristics often point to habitat and dietary context:

- **Grassland/shortgrass plains**
  - Fibrous, pellet-like droppings with abundant grass cuticle and stems.
  - Seeds from sedges and grasses common in late summer.
- **Mixed-grass/shrubland**
  - More heterogeneous contents with shrub fragments, small twigs, and leaves.
- **Riparian/wet meadow**
  - Higher moisture content in droppings; may contain wetland plant material and seeds of aquatic plants.
- **Seasonal signals**
  - Spring: fresh green material, higher moisture and softer consistency.
  - Summer: dry, fibrous material and numerous grass seeds.
  - Winter: lower volume, possibly concentrated and drier; browse items if available.
- **Dietary indicators**
  - High proportion of forbs and flowers → opportunistic foraging on available nutritious plants.
  - Large numbers of particular seed types → potential vector for plant dispersal; useful for plant–herbivore interaction studies.

## Ethics & safety

This section is essential — fieldwork involves living animals and potential biohazards.

- **Do not approach bison.**
  - Bison are large, unpredictable, and dangerous. Maintain safe distances per park/local regulations (often 25–100+ meters).
- **Minimize disturbance.**
  - Use binoculars, long lenses, and avoid altering animal behavior or habitat.
- **Permits & permissions**
  - Obtain landowner permission and research permits where required for sampling, photography, or long-term monitoring.
- **Biosecurity & zoonosis precautions**
  - Wear disposable gloves when handling scat.
  - Use face protection if generating aerosols (rare for passive collection).
  - Store samples in sealed, labeled bags or tubes; transport on ice for lab analysis if required.
  - Decontaminate tools and surfaces with appropriate disinfectant (e.g., 10% bleach solution) after use.
  - Be aware of zoonotic risks (e.g., E. coli, Cryptosporidium, Brucella in some regions) and local livestock disease concerns.
- **Sample handling best practices**
  - Use sterile swabs for DNA or microbiome sampling when possible (non-destructive sampling).
  - Label each sample with unique ID, GPS, date/time, collector, and relevant field notes.
  - Follow cold-chain and chain-of-custody protocols if samples are for diagnostic testing.
- **Data ethics**
  - Respect privacy and cultural sensitivities if samples are collected on tribal or protected lands.
  - Share data responsibly and credit contributors; anonymize precise locations for sensitive populations when appropriate.

## Future work

Opportunities to expand and professionalize this study:

- Molecular analyses
  - DNA metabarcoding for diet composition.
  - Host genetics and population assignment from fecal DNA.
- Microbiome and health
  - Gut microbial community profiling to assess health, stress, and anthropogenic impacts.
- Stable isotopes
  - Isotope analysis for long-term diet and migratory behavior inference.
- Parasite surveys
  - Quantitative parasitology (egg/oocyst counts) and pathogen screening.
- Machine learning & citizen science
  - Build an annotated image dataset to train automated scat ID models and enable volunteer contributions.
- Standardized protocol
  - Develop an SOP for non-invasive scat sampling, metadata fields, and labeling templates for multi-site comparability.

If you’d like to contribute data, methods, or code (image classifiers, mapping tools, metadata parsers), please open an issue or pull request.
